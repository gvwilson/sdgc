# Race, Culture, and Harm

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Race Is Not a Biological Category

-   Human genetic variation does not cluster in the groupings that racial categories describe
-   Concept of biological race constructed in the 18th and 19th centuries
-   Race is a legal and social category
-   Effects are real even though biological basis is not
-   We apologize for stating the obvious, but this ugly idea has been making a comeback
    as racist politics re-emerges, and silence now reads as complicity

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Racial Categories Are Made by Governments

-   US census has changed its racial categories in every decade since 1790
-   People classified as non-white in 1900 (Italians, Irish, Eastern Europeans) reclassified as white by 1940
-   Racial categories created, revised, and abolished for administrative convenience
    -   1952 US Immigration and Nationality Act: Mexicans classified as white for immigration purposes, then non-white for labor purposes, depending on which classification served the government's interest [%b Kendi2016 OmiWinant2015 Wilkerson2020 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Colonial Censuses Hardened Fluid Hierarchies

-   Pre-colonial social hierarchies in Africa and South Asia were fluid, context-dependent, and locally negotiated
-   Colonial censuses required fixed categories for administration, taxation, and divide-and-rule politics
-   Resulting categories persisted long after the colonial period and structured post-independence violence
    -   Belgian colonial categorization of Hutu and Tutsi in Rwanda hardened a fluid social distinction into a fixed hereditary identity enforced with identity cards
    -   Classification system that later structured the 1994 genocide [%b Dallaire2005 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Neutral Systems in Unequal Worlds

-   A system that ignores race in a world structured by historical racial inequality reproduces that inequality
-   Equal treatment of unequal situations produces unequal outcomes
-   Claim of neutrality encodes the prior judgment that the existing distribution is fair
    -   A mortgage algorithm trained on historical loan data will reproduce the effects of historical redlining without ever including race as an input variable [%b Khera2019 Oneil2016 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## What Professional Taboos Protect

-   Every professional culture places certain topics outside legitimate concern
-   What is forbidden defines belonging: raising a taboo topic marks you as not understanding the culture
-   Taboos protect the group's self-understanding and insulate it from external criticism
    -   Medical culture: discussing errors in rounds is tabooed in many hospitals [%b Douglas2002 Gawande2009 Kohn1999 %]
    -   Yet error disclosure is the only mechanism for systemic learning

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Taboos Cluster Around Power

-   Tech culture taboos: questioning whether a product should be built, discussing compensation, organizing
-   Medical culture taboos: discussing errors publicly, questioning senior clinicians in front of patients
-   Topics most dangerous to power are most heavily tabooed
    -   Google fired engineer James Damore in 2017 for writing a memo questioning diversity programs
    -   Firing enforced the taboo on challenging the company's self-understanding regardless of memo's content [%b TorresGebru2024 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Framing Systemic Problems as Individual Failures

-   "A few bad actors" frames institutional failure as individual deviance
-   Framing serves professional self-regulation and resists external oversight
-   If always a few bad actors, the institution is never the problem
    -   Catholic Church's response to clergy sexual abuse for decades: treat each case as a failure of an individual priest
    -   Framing concealed systematic institutional protection of abusers [%b Dutton2013 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Deciding What Questions Are Worth Asking

-   Research agendas are political decisions made by people with interests
-   What topics receive funding, publication, and citation shapes what counts as knowledge
-   "We study what we study" is not a neutral description
-   It is a choice
    -   Research on health effects of poverty consistently underfunded relative to research on individual lifestyle choices, because the latter implies cheaper interventions [%b Michaels2008 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Documented Harm, Public Denial

-   Facebook's internal research documented Instagram's harm to teenage girls' mental health
-   Company denied any such finding while research sat in internal files
-   Organizations can document specific harms internally while maintaining public denial
    -   Tobacco industry's internal documents, released through 1990s litigation, showed companies knew cigarettes were addictive and carcinogenic decades before admitting it publicly [%b Hilts2003 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Executing Instructions Instead of Moral Standards

-   Remember the Milgram experiment [described earlier](@/slides/inside-orgs/)?
    Same shift into an agentic state happens in corporate settings
-   Engineers who built engagement algorithms were not indifferent to harm
-   They were operating in an institutional mode that located responsibility elsewhere
    -   Facebook product manager Frances Haugen testified that the company knew about harms and chose engagement over safety
    -   Each individual employee deferred to the institutional judgment [%b Arendt2006 Bandura1999 Ehmke2025 Frenkel2021 Palazzo2025 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## [%g normalization_of_deviance "Normalization of Deviance" %]

-   Vaughan's analysis of the Challenger disaster: safety anomalies normalized through repeated near-misses
-   By the time of the launch decision, the O-ring failure mode had been accepted as within normal risk parameters
-   Risk thresholds shift gradually
-   Each deviation makes the next one seem smaller
    -   NASA engineers flagged the O-ring problem in writing seven times before the Challenger launch
    -   Each time reviewed and accepted as within acceptable risk [%b Vaughan1996 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## "We Just Build Tools" Doesn't Hold

-   A hammer is a tool
-   A recommendation algorithm that selects content to maximize engagement is not
-   Algorithm actively shapes behavior
-   Not passive infrastructure
-   "We just build tools" collapses when the tool is a decision-making system
    -   YouTube's recommendation algorithm documented in 2019 as the largest single driver of radicalization into extremist content
    -   Company responded by quietly adjusting recommendations rather than disclosing the finding [%b Frenkel2021 %]

</section>
