# Controlling Workers

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Structurally Unfree Labor

-   A labor relationship set by one party and enforced through debt is not free even when nominally voluntary
-   Southern US sharecropping after 1865 was formally free
-   Structurally it was not
    -   Landlords set the prices for seed and tools on credit and kept the accounts
    -   A good harvest reduced debt but the terms ensured a bad harvest increased it [%b Blackmon2008 Daniel1972 Devries2008 Thompson1963 %]
-   Formal freedom of contract does not guarantee actual freedom

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Debt That Perpetuates Itself

-   When the landlord sets prices and keeps the accounts, debt can grow regardless of effort
-   Advances for seed, tools, and food charged at inflated rates
-   Good harvest reduced debt
-   Terms designed so that a bad harvest increased it
    -   Debt peonage kept sharecroppers legally bound to the land in states that used vagrancy laws to enforce the arrangement

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## The Gig Economy as Putting-Out System

-   Platform gig workers own their tools but the platform owns the product and the customer relationship
-   Algorithmic changes and paid-promotion requirements reproduce the sharecropper's logic
    -   A food delivery driver penalized for low ratings has no appeal process and no employer to hold accountable
    -   The algorithm is the landlord
-   Putting-out system was the dominant form of manufacturing before the factory

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## When the Employer Controls Everything

-   Company towns provided housing, retail, banking, and community infrastructure
-   Workers who left lost not just a job but their home, credit, and community
-   Pullman in Illinois, coal towns in Wales, and mining camps in South Africa all used this model
    -   Pullman deducted rent, utilities, and store purchases from wages before workers saw any cash
    -   Making strike action financially catastrophic [%b Lindsey1942 Stoller2019 Walder2017 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Super-Apps as Company Towns

-   WeChat and similar super-apps integrate payments, communication, services, and social life
-   Suspension removes not just a communication tool but access to civic and commercial life
    -   In China, WeChat suspension has been used to enforce political compliance because the app is required infrastructure for daily transactions
-   Exit cost is deliberately raised through integration

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Lock-In Through Accumulated Investment

-   [%g structural_lock_in "Structural lock-in" %] differs from short-term dependency
-   Workers and businesses invest in platform-specific skills, reviews, and relationships
-   Each incremental worsening is tolerable because the exit cost exceeds the immediate harm
    -   An Uber driver with a 4.9-star rating cannot transfer that rating to Lyft
    -   Years of work stays with the platform

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Franchise Logic

-   Franchisors extract value through fees and rules while placing operational risk on franchisees
-   McDonald's profits from the system as a whole
-   Individual operators bear the costs of failure
    -   McDonald's earns most of its income from real estate: it owns the land and leases it to franchisees, giving it leverage over operators even beyond the franchise agreement [%b Schlosser2001 %]
-   Franchisor wins whether or not any individual franchisee succeeds

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## What App Store Fees Extract

-   15 to 30 percent commissions on all transactions within an app
-   Rules set unilaterally and changed on short notice
-   Violation results in removal from the only distribution channel for the platform
    -   Epic Games was removed from the App Store in 2020 for offering direct payment
    -   Case took four years in court and Epic still had to return to Apple's terms

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Reputation That Cannot Be Transferred

-   Reviews, ratings, and audience built on a platform belong to the platform, not the creator
-   A seller who leaves Amazon or an artist who leaves Spotify leaves their reputation behind
-   Lock-in persists after value extraction begins
    -   An Etsy seller with 5,000 five-star reviews cannot move those reviews to a competing marketplace
    -   Starting over means starting from zero

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## What [%g collective_bargaining "Collective Bargaining" %] Actually Achieves

-   Eight-hour day, the two-day weekend, workplace safety, prohibition of child labor
-   These were not conceded voluntarily
-   They were won through sustained collective action
-   Individual bargaining consistently produces worse outcomes than collective bargaining
    -   Countries with higher union density show smaller wage gaps between the highest- and lowest-paid workers [%b Kelly2012 Kelly2022 Silver2003 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## A Brief History of Labor Violence

-   Tolpuddle Martyrs (1834) were transported to Australia for forming an agricultural union
-   Ludlow Massacre (1914): Colorado National Guard fired on striking miners and their families
-   State and private violence against labor organizing is not ancient history
    -   Pinkerton Detective Agency employed tens of thousands of strikebreakers and infiltrators for corporations between the 1870s and the 1930s

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Codetermination in Practice

-   German [%g mitbestimmung "Mitbestimmung" %] requires worker representatives on supervisory boards
-   Workers in firms with over 2,000 employees hold half the board seats
-   Formal institutional authority, not advisory participation
    -   German codetermination credited with more conservative financial decisions, lower CEO-to-worker pay ratios, and higher investment in training [%b Gordon2001 Kelly2012 Thelen1992 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## The Passion Trap

-   Framing work as personal fulfillment legitimizes lower wages and longer hours
-   "Do what you love" makes exploitation feel like a choice
-   Passion principle most intense in exactly the sectors with the worst working conditions
    -   Unpaid internships most common in creative industries, NGOs, and tech startups
    -   Sectors where passion is used as a substitute for wages [%b Cech2021 Jaffe2021 Macintyre2020 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## What GDP Doesn't Count

-   GDP excludes unpaid domestic and care labor by deliberate design, not oversight
-   Exclusion has concrete consequences: women's pensions, credit access, and political standing
-   What is not measured is not managed
    -   Marilyn Waring documented in 1988 how the System of National Accounts was designed to exclude the labor that sustains households and communities [%b Waring1988 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## The "Housewife" as Industrial Policy

-   Full-time housewife role is a modern construction assembled under industrial policy
-   Japan's postwar tax system incentivized women to work part-time and remain economically dependent
-   Germany's joint taxation system had similar effects
-   Both were deliberate policy choices, not tradition
    -   Japan's "103 million yen wall" kept millions of women in part-time work for decades through tax thresholds designed to maintain dependent spouse arrangements

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## The Second Shift

-   Women in dual-income households perform roughly a month of additional unpaid labor per year
-   They tend to attribute this to personal preference rather than structural inequality
-   Inequality persists across income levels and educational backgrounds
    -   Arlie Hochschild coined the term "second shift" in 1989 to describe this pattern
    -   It has changed surprisingly little in the decades since [%b HochschildMachung1989 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## The Iceland Strike of 1975

-   90% of Icelandic women refused all paid and unpaid work for one day
-   Telephone exchanges, fish-processing plants, and nursery schools closed
-   Strike demonstrated in concrete terms what unpaid labor actually costs
    -   Same day became known as "the long Friday" because so many men had to bring their children to work

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Making Invisible Labor Visible

-   Silvia Federici: enclosing reproductive and domestic labor inside the household was an economic project
-   Platform care-work apps formalize informal feminized labor while preserving informal wages
-   Political change requires making the invisible visible first
    -   1975 Iceland strike showed that a single day of withdrawal could bring a country to a standstill [%b Federici2004 %]

</section>
