# Surveillance, Identity, and Desire

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Privacy as a Political Category

-   What counts as private has expanded and contracted through political contests
-   Privacy is not a timeless natural right
-   It is a historically contingent political achievement
-   Understanding this explains why it is fragile
    -   In the United States, the right to marital privacy was not established by the Supreme Court until Griswold v. Connecticut in 1965 [%b Igo2020 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## What Mass Surveillance Enables

-   Stasi employed one informant per 63 East Germans at its peak
-   Mass surveillance does not require active use to produce control:
    awareness of observation changes behavior
-   Corporate surveillance at platform scale exceeds what any state has achieved
    -   NSA's PRISM program, revealed by Edward Snowden in 2013, collected data from nine major internet companies
    -   But the companies' own commercial surveillance dwarfed what PRISM captured [%b Funder2011 Schneier2023 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Platform Data Is Not Voluntary Consent

-   Legal theory treats data collection as consensual because users click "agree"
-   Consent that cannot meaningfully be refused is not consent in any functional sense
-   Information asymmetry between platform and user is total
    -   Average privacy policy takes 76 working days per year to read if applied to every site a person visits
    -   Meaningful consent is structurally impossible [%b Schneier2023 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Algorithms and Real-World Violence

-   Platform algorithms amplifying dehumanizing content have documented connections to real-world violence
-   Myanmar military's use of Facebook to coordinate anti-Rohingya violence is the clearest example
-   Connection between content and violence is empirical, not speculative
    -   UN investigators concluded in 2018 that Facebook played a "determining role" in the genocide
    -   The platform had no Burmese-language moderation team at the time [%b Frenkel2021 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Why States Need to Identify People

-   States tax, draft, and police people
-   All three require knowing who people are
-   Identification systems tend to expand beyond their original purpose
-   Same system that enables services enables control
    -   India's Aadhaar biometric identity system launched for welfare distribution
    -   Now used by banks, telecoms, and landlords to screen applicants [%b Cole2002 Khera2019 Torpey2000 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Legibility Has No Natural Limit

-   James Scott: modern state wants populations legible, visible, countable, comparable
-   Desire for legibility has no built-in stopping point
-   Every expansion of identification capability has been used for the purposes of whoever controls it
    -   German census records collected in 1933 were used by the Nazis to identify Jewish residents by neighborhood
    -   Legibility built for one purpose became infrastructure for genocide [%b Scott1998 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## The Colonial Origins of Modern Surveillance

-   Fingerprinting developed by Francis Galton and Edward Henry to identify colonial subjects in India
-   Facial recognition and predictive policing developed on populations with the least power to resist
-   Tools refined on colonized people before being deployed on everyone
    -   Alphonse Bertillon's anthropometric identification system first tested on prisoners and colonial subjects in the 1880s before becoming standard police practice worldwide [%b Breckenridge2016 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Biometrics and New Exclusions

-   Biometric identification creates new forms of exclusion alongside the access it provides
-   People without biometric data (infants, some elderly, some disabled) cannot access systems requiring it
-   Error rates are not uniform across populations
    -   MIT Media Lab research found commercial facial recognition systems had error rates up to 34% for dark-skinned women compared to under 1% for light-skinned men [%b Browne2015 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Manufacturing Insecurity Is a Business Model

-   Beauty industry does not sell beauty
-   It sells relief from manufactured anxiety about appearance
-   Customer is never meant to arrive at satisfaction
-   Perpetual dissatisfaction is structurally necessary
-   Naomi Wolf documented this before social media existed
-   Platforms have scaled the mechanism
    -   Instagram's own internal research found that 32% of teenage girls said that when they felt bad about their bodies, Instagram made them feel worse [%b Frenkel2021 Packard2007 Peiss2011 Veblen1899 Wu2016 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Perpetual Dissatisfaction by Design

-   Social comparison anxiety is not a side effect of platform design
-   It is the mechanism
-   Platforms produce comparison at industrial scale and sell remedies for the anxiety they create
-   Business model requires the problem not to be solved
    -   Facebook's VP of product warned internally in 2019 that the company was "actively making the world worse"
    -   Memo circulated for months before anyone acted [%b Frenkel2021 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Social Comparison at Industrial Scale

-   Pre-platform comparison was bounded by proximity: neighborhood, school, workplace
-   Platforms expose users to an unlimited feed of aspirational content optimized for engagement
-   Comparison set is now global and curated to maximize dissatisfaction
    -   Norwegian tax transparency study found a 29% widening of well-being gaps when people could easily compare incomes [%b PerezTruglia2020 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Where Preferences Come From

-   Preferences are not given
-   They are shaped by social position, advertising, and available options
-   Status goods are valuable because they signal rank, not because of their intrinsic qualities
-   Production creates desires it then satisfies
    -   John Kenneth Galbraith called this the "dependence effect" in 1958: producers create the wants they then satisfy, making the market's claim to serve consumer preferences circular [%b Bernays2024 Galbraith1954 Galbraith1998 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Algorithms Shape What We Want

-   Recommendation systems surface content similar to prior engagement
-   Over time, they narrow the taste profile rather than expanding it
-   Filter bubble is a preference construction mechanism, not just a selection mechanism
    -   YouTube's recommendation algorithm documented as pushing viewers from mainstream political content toward progressively more extreme content through a series of individually small steps [%b Frenkel2021 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Markets Satisfy Preferences; They Don't Evaluate Them

-   Markets are efficient at giving people what they want
-   They cannot determine whether the preferences are worth having,
    who created them, or at whose expense
-   This is a limit of markets, not a moral failing of individuals
    -   Amartya Sen's capabilities approach asks not just whether preferences are satisfied but whether people had the realistic freedom to form preferences worth having [%b Singer1981 %]

</section>
