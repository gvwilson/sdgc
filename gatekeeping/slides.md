# Media and Gatekeeping

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Advertisers Buy Audiences, Not Readers

-   When audiences are the product and advertisers are the customer,
    the incentive is to assemble the audience advertisers want
-   Penny press of the 1830s made news cheap by selling readers to advertisers
-   Same structure as social media platforms
    -   New York Sun (1833): one cent a copy (below cost), revenue from selling readers to advertisers [%b Campbell2001 Herman1988 Packard2007 Wu2016 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Inflammatory Journalism Is Rational

-   Outrage, fear, and moral disgust hold attention longer than neutral information
-   For an attention-funded business, this is a rational editorial strategy, not a moral failure
-   Hearst and the Spanish-American War
-   Cable news and crime coverage
    -   Hearst's papers ran sensationalized Cuba coverage in 1898 that historians credit with helping push the US into a war that benefited his circulation figures [%b Nasaw2000 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## The Algorithmic Penny Press

-   Recommendation algorithms extend the penny press logic to individual content units
-   Every piece of content evaluated for engagement, not accuracy or social value
-   Outrage and social harm are inseparable when outrage generates the strongest signal
    -   Internal Facebook research: content generating "angry" reactions distributed five times more widely than content generating other reactions [%b Frenkel2021 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## What Actually Happens in Disasters

-   Disasters consistently produce mutual aid, improvised cooperation, and increased trust
-   People share resources with strangers, form impromptu organizations, help those they don't know
-   Well documented across earthquakes, floods, hurricanes, and industrial accidents
    -   Looting after disasters is reliably rare and minor
    -   "Looting" that does occur is often survival behavior like taking food from abandoned stores [%b Solnit2009 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Prosocial Behavior Is the Norm

-   Rebecca Solnit documented this across the 1906 San Francisco earthquake,
    the 1985 Mexico City earthquake, and the aftermath of 9/11
-   The exception is not altruism
-   The exception is antisocial breakdown
-   Media coverage consistently describes the opposite because cooperation is not dramatic
    -   Mexico City earthquake produced hundreds of neighborhood mutual aid organizations that outlasted the immediate crisis and became permanent civic infrastructure [%b Solnit2009 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Elite Panic and Its Costs

-   Authorities fearing population breakdown militarize relief in ways that cause additional harm
-   Response to Hurricane Katrina involved shooting people who were mostly trying to survive
-   "Elite panic": those in power fear the population more than the disaster
    -   In New Orleans in 2005, police shot unarmed people on the Danziger Bridge who were walking to a store for food [%b Solnit2009 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## The Price of False Narratives

-   False narratives of social breakdown delay evacuation, turn back relief, and justify violence
-   Costs measured in lives, not in editorial judgment
-   Accurate coverage of disaster behavior saves lives
    -   After the 2010 Haiti earthquake, false reports of gang violence led international aid convoys to turn back from areas that actually needed help [%b Quarantelli1998 Tierney2006 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Why Cooperation Isn't News

-   A neighbor helping a neighbor is not an event
-   A neighbor shooting a neighbor is
-   Dramatic conflict has a structural advantage in attention-funded media
-   Produces a systematically distorted picture of human nature
    -   Solnit found that "looting" appeared hundreds of times more often than "mutual aid" in disaster coverage despite mutual aid being far more common [%b Solnit2009 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## When Placement Is for Sale

-   A gatekeeper profiting from preferential placement is no longer signaling quality
-   Prominent placement becomes a signal of payment, not merit
-   Radio payola: DJs accepted cash to play specific songs while presenting choices as editorial
    -   Alan Freed, who popularized "rock and roll" and championed early Black artists, was indicted in the 1960 payola scandal and never recovered his career [%b Segrave1994 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Payola and Platform Search Rankings

-   Google Search mixes paid results with organic results in ways that obscure the distinction
-   Amazon search rankings reflect both paid placement and organic relevance
-   Conflict of interest identical to 1950s radio
-   Mechanism more opaque
    -   Amazon has used search rankings to favor its own private-label products over independent sellers, as documented in a 2019 Wall Street Journal investigation [%b Doctorow2022 Khan2017 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Regulatory Capture Explains Why It Persists

-   Recording industry cared intensely about payola regulation
-   Listeners did not
-   The better-organized side consistently wins regulatory contests
-   As we will see [later](@/slides/capture-fascism/),
    diffuse public harm is structurally disadvantaged against concentrated industry interest

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Structural Separation as the Only Fix

-   Structural separation means the entity controlling access derives no revenue from placement
-   Voluntary disclosure is insufficient: incentive to obscure remains
-   EU Digital Markets Act begins to require separation
    -   Separating Google's search business from its advertising business would eliminate the incentive to favor advertisers in organic results
    -   Which is why the company has fought it [%b Stoller2019 %]

</section>
