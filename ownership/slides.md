# Who Owns What

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Intellectual Property Is Not a Natural Right

-   Copyright, patent, and trademark are legal instruments with specific economic purposes
-   Created by legislatures and can be changed by legislatures
-   Natural rights framing is a lobby position, not a legal description
    -   "You wouldn't download a car" treats IP as equivalent to physical property
    -   The exact legal fiction the campaign defends [%b Baldwin2014 Bellos2024 Bracha2019 Wu2010 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Copyright and Patent Terms Keep Extending

-   US copyright was 14 years in 1790 and is now life plus 70 years
-   Extensions have consistently served incumbent content industries, not creators
-   Primary beneficiary of the Sonny Bono Copyright Term Extension Act was Disney, not songwriters
    -   Act passed in 1998 just as Mickey Mouse cartoons were about to enter the public domain [%b Wu2010 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Public Research, Private Patents

-   Publicly funded research is routinely patented by private companies
-   Bayh-Dole Act (1980) allowed universities and companies to patent federally funded discoveries
    -   HIV drug AZT developed with NIH funding
    -   Burroughs Wellcome received the patent and priced it at $10,000 per patient per year when introduced in 1987 [%b Mazzucato2013 %]
-   Results priced as if the public subsidy did not happen

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## What Open Source Reclaims—and What It Doesn't

-   Open source licenses prevent proprietary capture of the code itself
-   Large firms extract value through cloud services, proprietary extensions, and support contracts
    -   Amazon's AWS built a business worth over $90B annually on open-source Linux and other software without proportionally contributing back to the projects it depends on [%b Doctorow2022 Srnicek2016 %]
-   Commons partially reclaimed
-   Extraction continues by other means

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Controlling a Standard Controls a Market

-   Everyone who builds on a standard becomes dependent on whoever sets it
-   USB-C, HTML, and TCP/IP are standards with very different governance structures
    -   Microsoft's dominance in the 1990s rested largely on controlling the Windows API standard
    -   Applications written for Windows would not run on competing operating systems [%b Russell2014 Shapiro1999 Wu2010 %]
-   Standard-setting is a form of market power, not a neutral technical exercise

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## The FRAND Problem

-   Standards-essential patents must be licensed on fair, reasonable, and non-discriminatory terms
-   The word "reasonable" has funded decades of litigation
-   FRAND commitments do not prevent extraction
-   They make it slower
    -   Qualcomm was found to have violated FRAND commitments for its modem patents, but litigation ran for over a decade before courts imposed any remedy

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Open vs. Proprietary APIs: A One-Sided Bet

-   Building on a proprietary API is a bet that the platform will not change its terms
-   Historical record makes this a bad bet
-   Twitter's API closure and App Store rule changes are recent examples
    -   Google closed its Reader RSS product in 2013, destroying the ecosystem that had grown around it with no warning to dependent developers

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## The Internet Was Built with Public Money

-   ARPANET, TCP/IP, the web, GPS, and the touchscreen were developed with public funding
    -   Tim Berners-Lee invented the web at CERN, a European publicly funded research institution, and gave the protocols away without patents [%b Acemoglu2023 Chang2012 Mazzucato2013 %]
-   Semiconductor research, battery technology, and pharmaceutical compounds follow the same pattern
-   Private sector commercialized what the public sector built

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Privatizing Gains, Socializing Losses

-   Risk borne collectively through public investment
-   Profit captured privately through patents, licensing, and market power
    -   When major banks failed in 2008, governments provided trillions in bailouts while executives collected bonuses from those same funds [%b JohnsonKwak2010 %]
-   Asymmetry is a policy choice, not a market outcome

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## How Transfer Pricing Works

-   A multinational sets the price at which its subsidiaries trade with each other
-   Setting high prices for intangible assets in low-tax jurisdictions shifts profit offshore
    -   Apple routes profits through Irish subsidiaries that paid effective tax rates below 1%, using licensing fees for intellectual property that Apple values at prices it sets itself [%b Shaxson2011 Zucman2015 %]
-   Legal tax avoidance, not illegal evasion

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Why the Old Rules Don't Fit Intangible Assets

-   Transfer pricing rules were designed for physical goods with observable market prices
-   Intangible assets (software, trademarks, data) have no comparable market price
    -   Amazon can value the "Alexa" trademark at any internal price when licensing it to a subsidiary, and there is no independent comparable transaction to check against [%b Schneier2023 %]
-   Ambiguity exploited by design

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## The Minimum Tax Project

-   OECD's Pillar Two establishes a 15% global minimum corporate tax
-   Implementation requires each country to pass domestic legislation
    -   Ireland, which built its economy partly on being a low-tax destination for multinationals, spent years lobbying against the minimum tax that would reduce its competitive advantage
-   Incumbent industries lobby against implementation in every jurisdiction

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Resource Sovereignty in Practice

-   Bolivia nationalized its natural gas industry in 2006 under President Evo Morales
-   State revenues from gas exports redirected to social programs: pensions, literacy campaigns, and cash transfers
-   Poverty fell by more than half over the following decade
-   Nationalization showed that resource ownership is a political choice with measurable consequences [%b Webber2011 %]

</section>
