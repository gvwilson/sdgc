# Harm, Morality, and Empathy

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Industrial Harm Has an Address

-   Communities near chemical plants, mines, smelters, and data center cooling systems
    bear costs that others don't
-   Environmental injustice is geographic: proximity to industrial harm correlates with race and income
-   Sacrifice zones are a policy outcome, not an accident of geography
    -   Robert Bullard documented in 1990 that hazardous waste facilities in the US South were sited almost exclusively in Black communities
    -   Pattern was deliberate rather than coincidental [%b Nixon2011 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Relocation Is Not Reduction

-   Stronger regulation in one jurisdiction relocates harm rather than reducing it
    when production can move without losing market access
-   Basel Convention was partly an attempt to prevent toxic waste from being shipped
    to countries with weaker rules
-   Harm continues
-   It happens somewhere else
    -   After the 1984 Bhopal disaster, Union Carbide did not upgrade its US plants to the same safety standards it had failed to maintain in India [%b Lapierre2002 Nixon2011 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Who Pays for What Others Consume

-   Communities absorbing production costs are rarely the communities consuming the output
-   Cobalt for electric vehicle batteries mined in the Democratic Republic of Congo
    under conditions few buyers see
-   Physical supply chain exists regardless of whether the buyer acknowledges it
    -   Amnesty International documented children as young as seven mining cobalt in artisanal mines supplying major battery manufacturers in 2016 [%b Nixon2011 Patel2017 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Tech's Supply Chain Is Not Invisible

-   Data centers require cooling water, electricity, and land
-   Device manufacturing requires rare earths, assembly labor, and chemical processing
-   "The cloud" is always somewhere
    -   A single large language model training run consumes as much water as 700,000 Americans use in a day
    -   Data centers overwhelmingly located in places where water is scarce [%b TorresGebru2024 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## What Appears Ancient Is Often Recent Construction

-   Legal prohibitions described as "reflecting traditional values" are often colonial-era impositions
-   Same-sex relationships criminalized across the British Empire through the Indian Penal Code of 1860
-   The law was not ancient
-   Imposed by the colonizing power on populations with more varied practices
    -   Section 377, written by Thomas Macaulay in 1860, criminalized "carnal intercourse against the order of nature"
    -   Partially struck down by India's Supreme Court only in 2018 [%b Charrad2001 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Social Movements Produce Legal Change

-   Moral consensus shifts through sustained organizing, legal strategy, coalition building, and political pressure
-   Moral argument alone has a poor track record as the primary lever
-   Argument addresses sympathetic audiences
-   Does not move hostile ones
    -   US civil rights movement combined moral argument with economic disruption (boycotts), legal strategy (test cases), and political pressure (voter registration)
    -   Each was necessary [%b Chenoweth2011 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Each Step Makes the Next Thinkable

-   Legal change tends to be incremental: the first step redefines what is possible
-   Civil unions before marriage equality
-   Decriminalization before recognition
-   Reform before repeal
-   Each incremental change makes the next one imaginable to audiences who would not have accepted it earlier
    -   Denmark legalized registered partnerships in 1989: first country to do so [%b Dowbiggin2007 Gawande2014 Hirshman2012 Lewy2011 %]
    -   Debate made marriage equality conceivable in countries that would have rejected it outright

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Changed Facts Change the Political Calculation

-   Political calculus shifts when facts change: more people know openly gay people than did in 1970
-   Changed visibility alters who is affected by a policy, and therefore who will oppose it
-   Moral argument addresses sympathizers
-   Changed facts affect opponents differently
    -   "It Gets Better" project, launched in 2010, made queer experience visible to people who had never encountered it directly
    -   Produced a measurable shift in polling on marriage equality [%b Allport1954 Faderman2015 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## The Circle of Moral Concern Has Expanded—Unevenly

-   Range of beings recognized as deserving moral consideration has grown over centuries
-   Expansion has not been smooth
-   Has included catastrophic reversals
-   Philosophical argument for expansion and political history of expansion are not the same story
    -   Peter Singer's 1981 argument that the circle of moral concern should expand to include all sentient beings remains philosophically contested
    -   But practical expansion of legal rights has proceeded through political organizing, not philosophy [%b Singer1981 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Human Rights as a 1970s Construction

-   Human rights framework as it now operates emerged in the 1970s, not the 1940s
-   1948 Universal Declaration was largely ignored for decades
-   Rights discourse expanded into a major political language after other political utopias had failed
    -   Samuel Moyn's 2010 book *The Last Utopia* documented that human rights became a dominant framework in the late 1970s specifically because socialist internationalism had collapsed as an alternative [%b Moyn2010 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Narrative as Political Strategy

-   Harriet Beecher Stowe's Uncle Tom's Cabin: Abraham Lincoln credited her with starting the Civil War
-   This is an overstatement
-   It points to something real about how narrative builds political capacity
-   Fiction trains readers to inhabit other inner lives
-   Deployed deliberately as political strategy
    -   Uncle Tom's Cabin sold 300,000 copies in its first year (1852) and was translated into 60 languages
    -   Made the experience of enslavement legible to people who had never encountered it directly [%b Hochschild2006 Hunt2007 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Gains Can Be Reversed

-   Expansion of rights is never linear
-   Gains can be reversed through violence,
    judicial capitulation, and political manipulation
-   US Supreme Court's reversal of Roe v. Wade (2022) is the clearest recent example
-   Treating gains as permanent is the error that makes reversal possible
    -   Right to vote for Black Americans effectively nullified between 1877 and 1965 through legal mechanisms and violence
    -   Reversal lasted longer than the right had existed [%b Anderson2018 Hannig2023 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Legal Change at a Founding Moment

-   Tunisia's Personal Status Code, enacted in 1956 in the same year as independence,
    abolished polygamy, established equal divorce rights, and required consent for marriage
-   Among the most progressive family law reforms in the post-colonial world
-   Enacted by a new state consolidating power, not through feminist lobbying
-   Showing how legal change can be structurally driven [%b Charrad2001 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Apparent Neutrality Encodes Prior Judgments

-   Every system making decisions about people embeds assumptions about whose interests count
-   A facial recognition system that performs poorly on dark skin is not neutral
-   It encodes a prior judgment about whose accuracy matters
-   "We don't take sides" is itself a side
    -   Twitter's 2016 decision to allow all political speech was framed as neutrality [%b Frenkel2021 %]
    -   Disproportionately benefited those whose political speech consisted of harassment

</section>
