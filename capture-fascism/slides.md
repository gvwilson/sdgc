# Capture, Control, and Fascism

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## How Regulatory Capture Works

-   George Stigler (1971): regulatory agencies come to serve the industries they regulate
-   Incentive asymmetry is structural: industry cares intensely, individual citizens care diffusely
-   Not primarily about corruption
-   About who is organized
    -   FAA approval process for Boeing 737 MAX gave Boeing's own employees authority to certify the aircraft, then approved it over internal safety objections [%b Stigler1971 Freudenberg2011 Michaels2008 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Concentrated Interest vs. Diffuse Harm

-   A regulatory change worth hundreds of millions to a handful of companies gives them powerful reasons to invest in influencing the outcome
-   Each individual citizen only slightly harmed by any given regulatory failure
-   Over time, the better-organized and better-funded side wins
    -   Financial industry: ~$500M/year on US lobbying, more than any other sector [%b JohnsonKwak2010 Lewis2010 Wu2010 %]
    -   Has shaped every major financial regulation since 2008

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## The [%g revolving_door "Revolving Door" %]

-   Regulatory agencies recruit from the industries they oversee because that is where expertise is
-   Those same employees know their next job may be back in industry at higher pay
-   Consequences of this incentive structure are predictable
    -   SEC enforcement chief who approved Goldman Sachs's mortgage products in 2006 took a senior position at Goldman in 2010 [%b JohnsonKwak2010 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Cognitive Capture

-   Regulators can come to share the industry's models, assumptions, and language without being paid off
-   Alan Greenspan: "I found a flaw in my belief that banks would self-regulate to protect their own interests"
-   Cognitive capture more durable than financial capture because it does not feel like corruption
    -   Efficient markets hypothesis dominated academic finance and regulatory thinking for three decades
    -   Economists who challenged it were sidelined not because they were wrong but because the hypothesis served ideological purposes [%b JohnsonKwak2010 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## When Capture Is Reversible

-   Capture correlates with degree of public attention to an agency's work
-   Career structures matter: rotating staff reduces capture
-   Entrenchment increases it
-   Capture partially reversed after Deepwater Horizon
-   Reversal was incomplete
    -   Minerals Management Service, which had waived safety regulations for the blowout preventer, was renamed and reorganized after the disaster
    -   Core personnel remained

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Legibility and Its Costs

-   As described [earlier](@/slides/surveillance/),
    James Scott: modern states want populations legible, countable, standardized
-   Standardization serves the center, not the periphery
-   What can be measured can be managed
-   What cannot be measured is eliminated
    -   McNamara's use of body counts as the metric of success in Vietnam produced a war optimized for body counts rather than strategic outcomes [%b Scott1998 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## What Top-Down Rationalization Destroys

-   Collectivization of Soviet agriculture destroyed the local knowledge that made peasant farming work
-   High-modernist planning consistently underestimates the value of what it replaces
-   The legible version of an activity is rarely as productive as the messy original
    -   Scott's analysis of Tanzanian ujamaa villages: forced collectivization destroyed both productivity and community social structures that could not be reconstructed [%b Scott1998 Spufford2012 Phillips2019 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Internal Planned Economies Without Accountability

-   Large corporations already operate internal planned economies with information advantages
-   Unlike mid-century socialist states, they have better information technology and no obligation to serve public purposes
-   Critique of central planning applies to corporate hierarchies as readily as to states
    -   Walmart's internal supply chain coordination more centralized than most countries' economic planning
    -   Optimized for profit rather than social welfare [%b Scott1998 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Tech Anti-Democratic Ideology

-   Significant strand of tech ideology holds that democratic institutions are too slow and too responsive to short-term preferences
-   Peter Thiel (2009): "I no longer believe that freedom and democracy are compatible"
-   Curtis Yarvin: the US should be governed by an unelected, unaccountable CEO-like executive
    -   Both Thiel and Yarvin have direct relationships with senior Trump administration officials
    -   Yarvin's ideas circulate in policy circles under the label "neoreaction" [%b Andreessen2023 Robin2018 TorresGebru2024 Thiel2014 Cassidy2025 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## [%g longtermism "Longtermism" %] as Cover for Present Harms

-   Longtermism: the most important thing is to ensure the long-run future of civilization, potentially trillions of people
-   Any policy causing harm now but increasing probability of that future is justified
-   Discounts present harms against speculative future goods with no falsification mechanism
    -   Same logic used to justify every utopian atrocity of the 20th century: present suffering acceptable because a perfect future was coming [%b Torres2024 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## What Fascism Actually Is

-   Robert Paxton: fascism defined by what it does, not what it says
-   Fascist features identifiable from action
-   Rhetoric varies by context
-   Mussolini's Italy, Hitler's Germany, and Franco's Spain shared structural features despite different rhetoric
    -   Paxton identified five stages: creation of movements, rooting in the political system, seizure of power, exercise of power, and long duration in which the regime normalizes violence [%b Paxton2004 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Key Features of Fascist Movements

-   Cult of national rebirth through purification and struggle
-   Obsessive focus on perceived humiliation combined with fantasies of domination
-   Violence treated as regenerative
-   Systematic dismantling of legal protections for targeted groups
    -   These features appear across fascist movements in Europe, Latin America, and Asia regardless of the specific nationalist mythology invoked [%b Paxton2004 Jaffrelot2023 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Law That Binds Without Protecting

-   Law in a fascist arrangement protects in-groups but does not bind them
-   Binds out-groups but does not protect them
-   Asymmetric application of law is the diagnostic feature
    -   Nuremberg Laws applied racial definitions to determine who had legal standing
    -   German law explicitly structured to protect some citizens and expose others [%b Paxton2004 Wilhoit2018 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Fascism Is a Style, Not an Ideology

-   Fascism can arrive in left rhetoric as easily as in right rhetoric
-   Pattern is not uniquely European
-   Recurs across different cultural and historical contexts
-   Identifying fascism requires attending to what movements do, not what they claim to represent
    -   Umberto Eco's 1995 essay "Ur-Fascism" identified 14 features of fascist thinking and found them present across movements that claimed incompatible ideologies [%b Eco1995 Black2001 %]

</section>
