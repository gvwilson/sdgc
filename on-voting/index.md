# Elections, Courts, and Democratic Erosion

Democratic systems do not fail all at once.
They erode gradually through legal mechanisms,
and the erosion often looks like ordinary politics until enough of it has accumulated to be irreversible.
Electoral systems, courts, campaign finance, and voter access interact
to determine not just who wins elections
but whether elections remain meaningful contests at all.
The patterns described here are visible across countries and across decades,
which is the first useful thing to understand about them:
they are not accidents.

## The Rules Shape the Outcome

The method used to count votes determines who wins as much as who votes.
[%g ranked_choice_voting "Ranked-choice voting" %],
[%g proportional_representation "proportional representation" %],
and [%g plurality_voting "plurality voting" %] produce different outcomes
from the same set of voter preferences.
This is not a technicality:
The choice of voting system determines which parties survive long enough to matter,
which coalitions form,
and how polarized the resulting legislature becomes.

The structural problem with plurality voting is that in a three-candidate race,
someone can win with 34 percent of the vote while 66 percent of voters preferred someone else.
This produces spoiler effects,
where a smaller party splits the vote of a larger ideological coalition,
and strategic voting,
where people vote for their second choice because they believe their first choice cannot win.

The 2024 UK general election illustrated the distortion.
The Green Party won approximately 7 percent of the national vote and received 4 of 650 seats in Parliament;
a proportional system would have given them roughly 46 seats.
The party that won a large parliamentary majority did so with 34 percent of the vote.
The result simply does not represent what voters wanted.

Countries using proportional representation have consistently higher voter turnout and lower polarization
than those using plurality systems [%b Achen2017 %].
New Zealand used [%g first_past_the_post "first-past-the-post" %] voting until 1996,
when it switched to a mixed-member proportional system after a national referendum.
Voter turnout rose,
and Parliament became more representative of the actual distribution of voter preferences.
Minor parties that had been squeezed out for decades gained seats.
No catastrophe followed.

[%g gerrymandering "Gerrymandering" %] compounds the problem wherever elections use geographic districts.
When a state legislature draws electoral boundaries,
the party in power can draw them to entrench its own advantage.
The technique has two moves:
[%g packing "packing" %] concentrates opposition voters into a small number of districts
they win by large margins,
wasting their surplus votes,
while [%g cracking "cracking" %] divides the remaining opposition voters across many districts
where they lose narrowly.
The result is a stable legislative majority that no single election can reverse.

After the 2010 US census,
Republican-controlled state legislatures used sophisticated mapping software
to draw districts with surgical precision.
In Wisconsin, Democrats won 51 percent of the state legislative vote in 2012
and received 39 percent of the seats.
North Carolina's congressional map was redrawn six times between 2010 and 2022,
each time after courts found unconstitutional partisan manipulation.
Each time,
the party in power drew a new map that preserved the same structural advantage under a different justification.

## Money, Suppression, and the Shrinking Coalition

In 2010, the US Supreme Court's *Citizens United v. FEC* ruling
held that political spending is a form of protected speech.
Corporations and wealthy donors could now spend unlimited sums through independent expenditure committees.
The decision did not create the influence of money in politics,
but it formalized and expanded it.
A small number of large donors now shape which legislation gets proposed
and which issues appear on legislative calendars
more directly than millions of ordinary voters casting ballots.

[%g dark_money "Dark money" %] extends this further.
Nonprofit organizations can spend on political advertising without disclosing their donors.
The organization funding a candidate can maintain public distance from
the positions their preferred candidates advance.
The result was that
a network of conservative nonprofits spent over $400 million influencing the 2012 US elections
without any public disclosure of the source [%b Mayer2016 %].
The same mechanism is available to any well-organized and well-funded interest group;
in practice, "well-organized" and "well-funded" gives the wealthy an enormous advantage.

Voter suppression operates through a different mechanism but toward a similar structural effect.
Reducing voting hours,
closing polling places in specific neighborhoods,
requiring particular forms of identification that some populations are less likely to hold,
and purging voter rolls are all legal in many jurisdictions.
These measures reduce turnout selectively:
they fall hardest on people with less flexible schedules,
less access to transportation,
and less geographic proximity to polling locations.
They do not require any official to have discriminatory intent:
they produce discriminatory outcomes through the design of the system [%b Anderson2018 %].
When the US Supreme Court gutted key provisions of the Voting Rights Act in 2013,
several states with documented histories of voting discrimination
implemented new restrictions within hours of the ruling.

## How Democracies Erode

Hungary, Turkey, and India all followed similar trajectories in the 2010s:
electoral victory followed by legal changes designed to entrench the winning party,
followed by pressure on courts, media, and civil society organizations.
Each step in this [%g democratic_backsliding "democratic backsliding" %]
can be described as a routine exercise of legislative or executive authority;
the pattern only becomes visible across time [%b LevitskyZiblatt2018 %].

Viktor Orbán's government in Hungary,
which came to power with a parliamentary supermajority in 2010,
used that supermajority to rewrite the constitution,
redraw electoral districts to its own advantage,
and install loyalists on the constitutional court—all within existing legal processes.
By the time the changes were complete,
Hungary had elections,
but the institutional ground on which opposition could mount a serious challenge had been removed.
The same sequence occurred in Venezuela, Turkey, and Peru,
and is happening right now in the United States.

Courts are political institutions.
They are staffed through political processes,
and their composition over decades reflects the sustained organizing of interested parties.
The Federalist Society,
a US conservative legal organization,
was founded in 1982 with seed funding from conservative donors.
Over the following four decades it built a systematic pipeline:
recruiting law students,
placing clerks,
and building a network of vetted candidates for judicial nominations.
By 2020,
all six conservative US Supreme Court justices were members or alumni.
The reversal of *Roe v. Wade* in 2022 was not a sudden event;
it was the culmination of a thirty-year project
that had been written out in detail in Federalist Society publications
years before the cases that accomplished it were filed.
Treating it as a surprise is a failure of historical analysis [%b Mayer2016 %].

The same structural concentration of power that characterizes democratic backsliding in states
appears in private governance.
Tech companies use dual-class share structures
to entrench founder control regardless of what shareholders prefer.
Mark Zuckerberg controls approximately 57 percent of Meta's voting power
while owning roughly 13 percent of its equity.
A founder with a minority economic stake can govern the company as an autocrat.
Private concentration of control over information flows for billions of people has public consequences,
but the legal frameworks to address it mostly do not yet exist.

## What Has Actually Worked

Alternatives to the dysfunctions described in this lesson exist and have been tried.
Ranked-choice voting in Ireland, Australia, New Zealand, and more than a dozen US cities
has not produced chaos:
it has produced legislatures that more closely reflect what voters actually want.
Independent redistricting commissions have measurably reduced partisan gerrymandering
in Arizona, California, and Michigan,
where independent bodies now draw district lines rather than the legislators who benefit from them.
New York City's public matching funds for small donations have shifted the incentive structure for candidates,
making it possible to run a competitive campaign on small contributions
rather than depending on a handful of major donors.
Broad constitutional reform through sustained public participation succeeded in Iceland
following the 2008 financial crisis.

The [research on mass movements](@/how-change/) applies here.
Sustained participation by roughly 3.5 percent of a population
has been sufficient to force political change in case after case.
Electoral organizing, legal challenges, constitutional campaigns,
and redistricting advocacy have all worked when that threshold of organized, sustained pressure was reached.
The rich and powerful will always resist;
while the specific tools differ,
sustained, organized pressure wins time after time [%b Young2024 %].
