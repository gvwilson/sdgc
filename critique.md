# Critique of Existing Lessons

## Overall Arc

The first eight lessons form a coherent sequence: how to see systems (seeing), what drives
individual behavior (behavior), how money and markets concentrate (monopoly), how
disruption actually works (disruption), who owns what (ownership), how workers are
controlled (worker-control), power dynamics inside organizations (inside-orgs), and what
alternatives exist (alternatives). This is the strongest stretch of the course — each
lesson builds on the last and the progression from individual psychology through market
structure to organizational power feels intentional.

After lesson 8, the arc frays. Lessons 9 through 18 (gatekeeping through moral-change)
read as a grab-bag of topics that could be reordered without losing much. The final four
lessons (regulation-works, how-change, ai-happens, on-voting) regain coherence as a
closing sequence on what works and what's coming, but the middle third needs
restructuring.

## Redundancy

### The tobacco doubt-manufacturing playbook appears in five or more places

- **seeing** Cognitive Pollution: tobacco, leaded gasoline, asbestos, oxycontin as
  examples of manufactured uncertainty.
- **regulation-works** How Tobacco Was Tamed: the same history in full detail, including
  the Master Settlement Agreement and Australia's plain-packaging fight.
- **how-change** The Ozone Hole That Closed: notes that the CFC industry "ran the same
  playbook" as tobacco.
- **ai-happens**: explicitly states that AI companies are using "the same one used by
  tobacco companies and the producers of leaded gasoline."
- **capture-fascism** Who Regulates the Regulators: the same pattern referenced in the
  context of cognitive capture.

The core narrative (industry funds doubt, delays regulation for decades, eventually loses
a partial victory) is told five times with varying depth. The telling in **regulation-works**
is the most complete and should be canonical; the others should cross-reference it rather
than re-narrate. As currently written, a reader who finishes the course will have
encountered the tobacco story so many times that it loses force.

### The "just" / invisible knapsack / lock-in cluster

- **inequality** Why Don't You Just: the invisible knapsack, cascading prerequisites,
  cognitive bandwidth.
- **worker-control** Big Tech is Like the Sharecropping System: platform lock-in as
  structural entrapment, the Pullman company town, the danwei.
- **inside-orgs** We're All Family Here: the family metaphor as a lock-in mechanism.

All three describe how systems make exit impossible while appearing voluntary. The
insights overlap but aren't cross-referenced. Consolidate the lock-in analysis in one
place and reference it from the others.

### Exploitation-structure analogies pile up

Across the course there are approximately eighteen section headers following the "Big Tech
is Like X" formula. A count by lesson:

- **disruption**: drug cartels, long firm fraud, enclosure movement
- **gatekeeping**: penny press, radio payola system
- **harmful-models**: professional wrestling, multi-level marketing, yakuza
- **institutions**: medieval Church, department store, credit bureau
- **leverage**: Scientology, ransom business
- **monopoly**: Standard Oil
- **surveillance**: Stasi, beauty industry
- **worker-control**: sharecropping system, company town, fast food franchise

The original critique correctly identifies the formula's diminishing returns but
understates the scale. Eighteen uses of the same framing device, across nearly every
lesson, is the actual problem. The best instances (drug cartels, sharecropping, the
medieval Church) work because the historical parallel illuminates mechanisms. The weaker
ones (wrestling, MLM, yakuza disaster-relief) stretch the analogy further than it can
bear. Consider reserving the "Big Tech is Like X" header only for cases where the parallel
is structural rather than rhetorical, and converting the rest to callouts or prose.

### Professional silence / self-regulation appears in two places

- **harm-culture** The Taboos of Professional Culture: medical errors, legal costs,
  software engineering books.
- **capture-fascism** Who Regulates the Regulators: cognitive capture of financial
  regulators.

Distinct enough to keep separate, but should explicitly reference each other.

### Status competition / manufactured desire appears in three places

- **behavior** Keeping Up with the Joneses: Veblen, positional goods, Frank's local-rank
  theory, social media as a status-anxiety machine.
- **surveillance** Big Tech is Like the Beauty Industry: manufactured insecurity, the
  beauty industry as a desire-manufacturing engine.
- **surveillance** How Do You Want What You Want?: Bernays, Galbraith's dependence
  effect, algorithmic preference shaping.

These three sections cover the same terrain from different angles and are spread across
two lessons that are otherwise about unrelated topics. They would work better consolidated
in one place, ideally adjacent to the behavioral economics material in **behavior**.

## Disjointedness

### Lessons whose sections don't connect

**gatekeeping** has three sections, not two: "Big Tech is Like the Penny Press"
(advertising-funded media), "A Paradise Built in Hell" (disaster altruism), and "Big Tech
is Like the Radio Payola System" (paid placement as editorial judgment). The Payola
section belongs with the Penny Press section — both are about advertisers corrupting
editorial judgment. The Paradise section is the outlier: it is excellent but belongs in a
lesson on collective action or human nature, not media economics.

**surveillance** has four sections: "Big Tech is Like the Stasi" (state surveillance),
"Who Are You?" (identity management, apartheid pass laws, Aadhaar), "Big Tech is Like the
Beauty Industry" (manufactured insecurity), and "How Do You Want What You Want?"
(manufactured preferences, Bernays). The first two sections share a coherent thread —
surveillance as a tool of control. The last two are about desire and preference shaping,
which is a related but distinct analysis. As written, the lesson's title and opening make
no promise that also covers manufactured desire, and a reader who came for surveillance
will be confused by the turn the lesson takes after "Who Are You?" Either split the lesson
or rename and reintroduce it so the transition is motivated.

**harmful-models** has three sections, not two: "Big Tech is Like Professional Wrestling"
(kayfabe), "Big Tech is Like Multi-Level Marketing," and "Big Tech is Like the Yakuza."
The Yakuza section is undermentioned in reviews of the lesson but is one of the stronger
analogies — the historical pattern of how Japan's anti-yakuza laws made political
connections a liability rather than an asset is a direct model for platform regulation.
It deserves more prominence, not less.

**behavior** has three sections, not two: "Why People Don't Maximize Utility"
(Kahneman/Tversky heuristics), "The Selfish Animal" (ultimatum game, Ostrom, the commons),
and "Keeping Up with the Joneses" (Veblen, positional goods, social media). The first two
critique *homo economicus* from different directions — cognition and motivation — but
never acknowledge each other; a bridging paragraph would help. The third section is
thematically important (it connects directly to what surveillance and behavior-modification
lessons build on later) but its relationship to the first two is left implicit. The lesson
ends on social media's weaponization of status anxiety, which is a strong conclusion, but
the reader has to supply the thread connecting all three sections themselves.

**speech-arms** covers four topics, not two: guns, free speech and hate speech, moral
panics and video games, and algorithmic criminal justice (COMPAS, facial recognition,
Aadhaar in welfare delivery). The connection between the first two (what the state permits
vs. restricts) is real but underexplained. The third and fourth sections are effectively
free-standing lessons. The video game panic section is carefully argued and its debunking
of the research literature is valuable, but it reads like an appendix to the free speech
discussion rather than a section of the same lesson. The COMPAS/facial recognition
section is substantive enough to anchor its own lesson on algorithmic decision-making;
burying it in **speech-arms** gives it inadequate visibility.

**moral-change** has three sections, not two: "The Geography of Industrial Harm"
(pollution havens, Bhopal, e-waste), "How Moral Consensus Changes" (LGBTQ+ rights,
assisted dying), and "The Expanding Circle" (the historical expansion of human rights from
slavery abolition through decolonization to disability rights, with Moyn's corrective
reading). The Expanding Circle section is one of the richest in the course but is easy to
miss because the lesson title — "Harm, Morality, and Empathy" — doesn't advertise it. The
environmental-justice opening and the rights-expansion closing feel like different lessons
because they are.

**monopoly** covers far more than the critique implies. It has four sections: "The
Creation of Money" (goldsmiths through fintech), "Big Tech is Like Standard Oil"
(monopoly formation), "Enshittification" (platform extraction cycles), and "Fads, Bubbles,
and the People Who Should Have Known Better" (tulip mania through AI valuation). The
Enshittification section is substantial and practically the course's most directly useful
lesson for understanding current platform dynamics. The Fads and Bubbles section concludes
with an analysis of AI as a speculative bubble — which directly anticipates **ai-happens**
and should either reference it or be relocated there to avoid the near-duplication.

**alternatives** is much longer and richer than the critique implied. In addition to the
corporate-forms taxonomy (which does read like a textbook), the lesson includes
"Alternatives That Scaled" (Mondragón, Raiffeisen, Vienna's Gemeindebau, Amul,
Desjardins — all substantial and well-narrated), "The Eight-Hour Day" (Melbourne
stonemasons, Haymarket, Triangle Shirtwaist, Kerala, Rana Plaza), and "Bullshit Jobs and
Parkinson's Law" (Keynes, Graeber, Google's Project Oxygen). The later sections are
engaging narrative. The critique's characterization of the lesson as a dry taxonomy was
based only on its first third.

### Sections that feel bolted on

- **inequality** The Myth of Meritocracy ends with a stray `[%b Cottom2017 %]` on
  line 75, floating outside any paragraph. Looks like a copy-paste remnant.

- **institutions** references `@/fastfood/` which does not exist as a lesson. The
  **worker-control** lesson has a substantial section titled "Big Tech is Like a Fast
  Food Franchise," suggesting the content was either split into worker-control or was
  originally intended as a standalone lesson. Either complete the fastfood lesson and
  make the reference valid, or rewrite the institutions cross-reference to point at the
  worker-control section.

- **seeing** Introduction is tonally anomalous. It is a personal, first-person manifesto
  ("I'm angry...so I teach") that reads like a foreword to a different project. The phrase
  "this book" appears in it. The rest of the course uses a consistent analytical register;
  this introduction does not. It should either be redesigned as a proper course preface or
  rewritten to match the tone of the material it introduces.

- **how-change** ends with an explicit "Conclusion" section that positions itself as the
  capstone to the entire course ("These essays have described how power is structured...").
  But **ai-happens** and **on-voting** come after it. Either the Conclusion section should
  move to after the final lesson, or **how-change** should not advertise itself as the
  ending of something that continues.

- Multiple lessons contain phrases like "an earlier post" (behavior), "the [behavioral
  economics post]" (surveillance), or "as described in [the lesson on disruption]"
  (ai-happens). The inconsistency between "post" and "lesson" framing suggests this
  material was originally a blog series and has not been fully edited for its current
  context. A systematic pass is needed to standardize the register.

## Places Likely to Lose Readers

### Over-long historical exposition before the tech payoff

- **monopoly** The Creation of Money: 140 lines of goldsmiths, fractional reserve
  banking, Graeber, and Jackson before reaching fintech and crypto. The history is good
  but the reader who came for tech critique may bail before the payoff.

- **worker-control**: the longest lesson by far. The sharecropping → peonage → platform
  lock-in sequence is strong, but the lesson also carries the full Eight-Hour Day history
  (now located in alternatives) and the Women's Work section. The Women's Work section
  is substantial (150+ lines) and covers GDP measurement, Iceland's 1975 strike,
  Hochschild's second shift, Federici, ASHAs — rich material that would anchor its own
  lesson on care work and invisible labor.

- **alternatives** The Usual Suspects / The Cooperative Tradition / Beyond the
  Anglo-American Tradition: roughly 100 lines of taxonomy before the Delaware flip
  discussion, which is lively. The earlier sections could be compressed or reorganized
  around the more engaging case studies in Alternatives That Scaled.

### Dense blocks without breaks

- **ai-happens** has no callout boxes and no `[%g ...]` glossary references — the only
  lesson with zero formatting variety. Every other lesson uses callouts to introduce
  supporting cases and historical asides; ai-happens is a continuous wall of paragraphs.
  Add callouts for supporting cases (the WGA strike, the NYT lawsuit, GDPR precedent).

- **on-voting** covers gerrymandering, campaign finance, voter suppression, democratic
  backsliding, and a constructive closing section ("What Has Actually Worked") in rapid
  succession with few breathing points. The closing section is valuable but easy to
  miss because the density of the preceding material exhausts attention before reaching
  it.

### The "Big Tech is Like X" formula tires

See the Redundancy section above. With approximately eighteen uses across the course, the
formula is overused enough to become background noise. The stronger instances — drug
cartels, sharecropping, the medieval Church, enshittification — work because the
historical parallel reveals mechanism. The weaker ones work rhetorically but don't add
analytical depth. Reserve the formula for structural parallels and convert the rest.

## Logical Thread

### What works

- Lessons 1–8 build a clear stack: perception → individual behavior → market structure →
  disruption → property → labor → organizational power → alternatives.
- Lessons 19–22 close well: regulation that worked → how change happens → AI-specific
  analysis → democratic erosion. This is a satisfying arc from evidence to application,
  though the Conclusion section in how-change (lesson 20) creates a false ending before
  the final two lessons.

### What doesn't

- Lessons 9–18 lack a discernible organizing principle. The reader moves from media
  economics to disaster sociology to surveillance to inequality to race to institutions
  to fascism to harmful models to organized crime to guns to free speech to video games
  to algorithmic criminal justice to pollution havens to moral change. The sequence
  feels like a shuffled deck.

  A possible reorder: group lessons by domain.
  - **Information and attention**: gatekeeping, surveillance, speech-arms (free speech
    and video games).
  - **Identity and harm**: harm-culture, inequality, moral-change (geography of harm),
    speech-arms (algorithmic criminal justice — could become its own lesson here).
  - **Institutional power**: institutions, capture-fascism, harmful-models,
    leverage, speech-arms (guns as institutional failure).

  Then the current final four would follow naturally as the "so what" sequence.

- **speech-arms** is trying to be four different lessons. The gun section, the free
  speech section, the moral panic/video game section, and the algorithmic criminal
  justice section each has enough material to stand alone. The current framing —
  "both guns and free speech are about what the state permits vs. restricts" — is real
  but insufficient to justify combining them. The COMPAS/facial recognition material
  especially deserves its own lesson or a home in a lesson on algorithmic harm.

- **moral-change** combines industrial harm geography, LGBTQ+ rights history, assisted
  dying, and the broad history of expanding the moral circle. The common thread is "how
  moral consensus shifts," but that thread is different enough in each section that the
  lesson reads as three separate essays that happen to share a title. The Expanding
  Circle section's argument — that rights expansion is contingent and reversible, not
  progressive — is the strongest conclusion available for the course and should be
  developed as such rather than buried in a lesson that opens with e-waste.

## Summary

The course has strong bones: the opening sequence (1–8) and closing sequence (19–22) are
well-structured and build momentum. The middle third needs the most work — redundancy
pruning, reordering for logical flow, and breaking up dense stretches with more callouts
and cross-references.

The tobacco doubt-manufacturing story is the single most over-repeated element: it appears
in at least five lessons. The "Big Tech is Like X" device appears approximately eighteen
times and has lost its rhetorical force through overuse; it should be reserved for cases
where the analogy is structurally illuminating, not merely vivid.

Several lessons are larger and richer than a casual reading suggests: **monopoly**
carries enshittification and bubble analysis; **alternatives** carries the full eight-hour
day history and the Graeber/Parkinson material; **surveillance** carries the beauty
industry and manufactured-desire sections; **speech-arms** carries video game panic and
algorithmic criminal justice. The course would benefit from an audit of whether all
section content is housed in the lesson that gives it the most visibility.

Seventeen specific copy errors need fixing, one broken internal link needs resolution, and
two sections (**seeing** Introduction, **how-change** Conclusion) need repositioning
relative to the course structure they imply.
