# Regulation That Worked

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Manufacturing Uncertainty About Scientific Consensus

-   As described [earlier](@/slides/seeing/),
    industries facing inconvenient science have funded counter-research to manufacture doubt
-   Goal was delay: if the science is uncertain, no action is required
-   Naomi Oreskes and Erik Conway documented this strategy across tobacco, acid rain, and climate change
    -   Same PR firm that managed the tobacco industry's doubt campaign, Hill and Knowlton, was hired by the Global Climate Coalition in the 1990s to manufacture uncertainty about climate science [%b Brandt2007 Oreskes2010 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Why No Single Lever Was Sufficient

-   Tobacco regulation required scientific research, litigation, journalism,
    and organized political pressure acting together
-   Each lever was necessary
-   None was sufficient
-   Durable change requires multiple simultaneous approaches
    -   1998 Master Settlement Agreement imposing restrictions on tobacco marketing came from state attorneys general litigation, not federal regulation
    -   Federal regulatory capture had blocked action for decades [%b Hilts2003 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## International Frameworks Accelerate Domestic Change

-   WHO Framework Convention on Tobacco Control (2003) established global norms
-   Countries with weak domestic tobacco industries could point to international consensus to justify regulation
-   Framework gave political cover to governments facing domestic industry opposition
    -   Over 180 countries have ratified the FCTC
    -   Countries that ratified it showed faster declines in smoking rates than those that did not [%b Hilts2003 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## "Tamed" Means Constrained, Not Eliminated

-   Tobacco is still a major industry
-   Still causes millions of deaths annually
-   Regulatory achievement was constraint: reduced prevalence, warning labels,
    advertising restrictions, and indoor bans
-   Success measured against the counterfactual, not against perfection
    -   US smoking rates fell from 42% in 1965 to 11% in 2023
    -   Reduction represents millions of deaths avoided, even as the tobacco industry remained profitable [%b Hilts2003 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Deaths Are Design Choices

-   Ralph Nader's 1965 book documented that vehicle safety deaths were a design and business choice
-   Cars designed without features that would absorb crash energy rather than transmit it to occupants
-   Technology to improve safety existed
-   The decision was not to use it
    -   Nader documented that Ford's internal calculations showed the Pinto's fuel tank design would cause 180 deaths and 180 serious injuries
    -   Company calculated that paying wrongful death settlements was cheaper than redesigning the tank [%b Nader1965 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## "User Error" as Corporate Strategy

-   Auto industry attributed crash deaths to driver failure for six decades
-   Framing directed attention away from vehicle design and toward individual behavior
-   Same framing recurs in every technology context where design choices cause harm
    -   Boeing attributed the 737 MAX crashes in 2018 and 2019 to pilot error before investigators established that the MCAS software system was the primary cause [%b Nader1965 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Surveilling the Critic Who Exposed You

-   General Motors hired private investigators to find damaging information about Ralph Nader
-   When the surveillance was exposed, it collapsed GM's credibility and accelerated Senate hearings
-   Surveillance was a greater political liability than the safety criticism it was intended to neutralize
    -   GM's president had to apologize to Nader personally before the Senate Commerce Committee in 1966
    -   Spectacle became the most effective advertisement for the legislation Nader was seeking [%b Nader1965 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Mandatory Safety Standards Work

-   National Traffic and Motor Vehicle Safety Act (1966) created federal safety standards for vehicles
-   Seatbelt requirements, airbag mandates, and crashworthiness standards followed
-   Traffic deaths per mile driven have fallen steadily
-   Industry's predictions of infeasibility were wrong
    -   Traffic fatalities per 100 million vehicle miles traveled fell from 5.5 in 1966 to 1.4 in 2019
    -   Industry's 1966 prediction was that safety requirements would "destroy the automobile industry" [%b Mashaw1990 Nader1965 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Industry's Infeasibility Predictions Have Been Wrong Again and Again

-   In every domain, industry has predicted that safety regulation would be technically infeasible or economically devastating
-   These predictions have been wrong again and again in the same direction:
    safety improvements achievable at lower cost than claimed
-   Predictions are arguments against regulation, not empirical assessments
    -   Clean Air Act's catalytic converter requirement described by the auto industry as impossible to achieve on the required timeline
    -   Every major manufacturer met it [%b Oreskes2010 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Industrial Harm to Shared Resources Is Reversible

-   Cuyahoga River in Cleveland caught fire in 1969
-   Now used for recreation
-   Thames was biologically dead in the 1950s
-   Now supports salmon
-   Reversibility is not guaranteed but achievable with sufficient regulatory capacity and political will
    -   Rhine River, heavily polluted by Swiss chemical industry accidents in the 1980s, was restored through an international commission that set enforceable standards for each country's discharges [%b Boyce2010 Nixon2011 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Cross-Border Pollution Requires Cross-Border Solutions

-   Acid rain from UK power stations killed Scandinavian forests and lakes
-   UK government initially denied responsibility
-   1979 Geneva Convention on Long-Range Transboundary Air Pollution established binding obligations
-   International cooperation on environmental harm is harder than domestic regulation but it has succeeded
    -   Sweden brought the international acid rain case to the UN environment conference in Stockholm in 1972
    -   It took seven years of diplomatic pressure to produce binding obligations on the UK [%b Boyce2010 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Pollution Stopped When Discharging It Became Costly

-   Mechanism for river cleanup was not moral suasion: it was enforceable regulation
-   Clean Water Act (1972) made discharging untreated pollution illegal and attached penalties
-   Firms stopped polluting when complying became cheaper than the legal consequences of not complying
    -   Cuyahoga cleanup cost roughly $1.5B over two decades
    -   Economic value of the restored river through recreation, tourism, and real estate has been estimated at ten times that amount [%b Boyce2010 Singer2023 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Public Health Through Redistribution

-   Kerala, India achieved life expectancy and infant mortality rates comparable to wealthy countries
    on a fraction of their income
-   Mechanism was land reform, universal literacy campaigns, and sustained investment
    in public health infrastructure
-   Countries do not need to be wealthy to achieve good health outcomes
-   They need to choose to invest in them [%b Dreze2013 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Debt Relief as Health Policy

-   Ghana qualified for HIPC debt relief in 2001 and redirected savings to eliminate user fees for healthcare
-   Access for low-income Ghanaians rose sharply as cost barriers were removed
-   Debt relief is health policy when the savings are redirected rather than absorbed [%b Whitfield2011 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Universal Coverage Under Political Opposition

-   Thailand achieved near-universal health coverage in 2001 by extending a subsidized scheme to the uninsured,
    financed through general taxation
-   Policy enacted under intense opposition from medical professional associations
-   Middle-income countries outside the standard US/UK story have achieved what wealthier countries claim is impossible [%b Savedoff2012 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Community Leadership in Public Health

-   Uganda reduced HIV prevalence from around 15% in the early 1990s to under 6% by 2000
-   Response combined government leadership with community education and open public discussion
    that most governments at the time refused to attempt
-   Success came not from external resources but from political will and community mobilization [%b Epstein2007 %]

</section>
