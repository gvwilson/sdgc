# What Changes and How

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## The Only Universally Ratified Environmental Agreement

-   The [%g montreal-protocol "Montreal Protocol" %] (1987) ratified by every country in the world
-   Only international environmental agreement to achieve universal ratification
-   Ozone layer is recovering
-   Without the protocol, it would have collapsed
    -   Ozone hole expected to fully close by 2065
    -   Protocol estimated to prevent 2 million cases of skin cancer annually [%b Parson2003 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Scientific Consensus Was Necessary but Not Sufficient

-   1974 Molina-Rowland paper predicting ozone depletion was scientifically sound
-   As [described earlier](@/slides/seeing/), chemical industry funded counter-research and delayed action for over a decade
-   Antarctic ozone hole discovered in 1985 made denial impossible
    -   Discovery of the Antarctic ozone hole was a genuine scientific surprise
    -   Models had predicted gradual depletion, not a dramatic seasonal hole [%b Roan1989 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Why Industry Changed Position

-   Du Pont reversed its opposition to CFC restrictions after developing profitable substitutes
-   Substitutes changed the cost-benefit calculation: compliance became cheaper than continued resistance
-   Industry opposition to environmental regulation reverses when complying becomes more profitable than resisting
    -   Du Pont had held CFC patents for decades and initially funded doubt research
    -   Once it developed HFCs as substitutes, it switched to supporting the protocol that would mandate its own products [%b Roan1989 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## The Multilateral Fund and Why It Matters

-   Montreal Protocol created a fund to transfer technology and money from wealthy to developing countries
-   Addressed the legitimate objection that developing countries should not bear costs for problems they did not create
-   Without this mechanism, universal ratification would not have been achieved
    -   India and China both insisted on delayed compliance schedules and technology transfers before ratifying
    -   The fund made those commitments credible [%b Parson2003 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Land Reform as a Direct Challenge to Concentrated Wealth

-   Land reform redistributes the most fundamental form of wealth directly and measurably
-   Among the most contested political projects in history precisely because the stakes are visible
-   Taiwan (1949-1953), South Korea (1949-1950), and Japan (1947-1950) all implemented successful reforms
    -   Taiwan's land reform reduced tenancy from 40% to under 15% of farm households within four years
    -   Agricultural productivity and rural incomes both rose immediately [%b Dreze2013 Studwell2013 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Why Speed and Durability Matter Together

-   Reforms implemented slowly gave landlords time to legally circumvent redistribution
-   Reforms implemented quickly but without institutional support collapsed when political conditions changed
-   Successful reforms were both rapid and embedded in new structures that made reversal costly
    -   Bolivia's 1952 land reform was rapid but created individual rather than collective tenure
    -   Subsequent governments were able to reverse parts of it as political conditions changed [%b Conquest1986 Studwell2013 Walder2017 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## The Conditions for Successful Reform

-   Successful land reform required peasant pressure, allied urban movements,
    and external circumstances that weakened landlord resistance
-   In Taiwan and Korea, the external circumstance was US Cold War strategy
-   Reform succeeded where it was politically irreversible
    -   US pressure for land reform in Taiwan and Korea motivated by fear that without it, communist organizing would succeed
    -   Same logic led to opposition to reform elsewhere when landlords were better anticommunist allies [%b Studwell2013 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## The Individual-Hero Narrative Is Wrong and Disabling

-   Rosa Parks was the secretary of the Montgomery NAACP chapter and had attended a civil rights training school
-   Montgomery Bus Boycott organized by the Montgomery Improvement Association and sustained by hundreds of people
-   Reduction of collective campaigns to individual heroes makes them inspiring and useless as models
    -   Three other Black women had been arrested on Montgomery buses before Parks
    -   The NAACP was waiting for a case with facts that would hold up in court [%b Beckerman2022 Orleck1995 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## What Successful Campaigns Share Across Contexts

-   Indian independence, Polish Solidarity, South African anti-apartheid, the British suffragette movement
-   Organizational discipline, sustained commitment across setbacks,
    and understanding of economic and political pressure points
-   Context varies
-   Structural features are consistent [%b Chenoweth2011 Lakey2018 %]
    -   Solidarity's success in Poland depended on alliances between industrial workers, Catholic clergy, and urban intellectuals
    -   Cross-class coalition is a consistent feature of successful campaigns [%b Chenoweth2011 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Nonviolent Campaigns Succeed Twice as Often

-   Chenoweth and Stephan analyzed 323 resistance campaigns between 1900 and 2006
-   Nonviolent campaigns succeeded roughly twice as often as violent ones
-   Threshold for nonviolent success was consistent: active participation by roughly 3.5% of the population
    -   No campaign in their dataset that achieved 3.5% participation failed
    -   Most succeeded with far fewer [%b Chenoweth2011 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Why Nonviolence Recruits Broader Participation

-   Nonviolent campaigns can recruit from a broader population,
    including people who will not take up arms
-   Broader participation creates broader [%g legitimacy "legitimacy" %]
-   Also makes repression harder to frame as protecting order rather than suppressing dissent
    -   Birmingham campaign in 1963 recruited children specifically because fire hoses and police dogs used against children produced images the Kennedy administration could not ignore [%b Faderman2015 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Economic Disruption Connects Pressure to Policy

-   Montgomery Bus Boycott worked because it destroyed the bus company's revenue from Black ridership
-   South African divestment campaign raised the cost of international borrowing for the apartheid state
-   Mechanism is economic: cost-benefit calculation for those in power changed
    -   Black riders accounted for 75% of Montgomery's bus revenue
    -   Boycott caused the company to lose money for over a year before the Supreme Court struck down segregated seating [%b Orleck1995 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Moral Suasion Without Pressure Consistently Fails

-   Slaveholders were not persuaded that slavery was wrong and then ended it
-   Tobacco industry did not voluntarily stop marketing to children because researchers documented harm
-   Moral argument builds coalitions
-   Economic and political pressure produces policy change
    -   Abolitionists made the moral case against slavery for decades with no legislative result
    -   Legal end of slavery in the US required a war [%b Chenoweth2011 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Mass Nonviolent Pressure Ending Authoritarian Rule

-   South Korea's June Democratic Uprising of 1987 driven by a coalition of students,
    workers, and middle-class citizens
-   Six weeks of sustained street protests forced the military government to accept direct presidential elections,
    ending decades of authoritarian rule
-   Succeeded because it cut across class lines and reached a scale that repression could not contain [%b Chenoweth2011 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Indigenous Political Agency and Legal Change

-   Indigenous Lumad and B'laan communities in the Philippines organized against a geothermal project
    on their ancestral lands in the 1990s
-   Won legal recognition of indigenous land rights that influenced subsequent Philippine environmental law
-   Outcome came from sustained political organizing, not from international intervention [%b Nixon2011 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Every Technical Decision Is a Political Decision

-   As we said [at the start](@/slides/seeing/),
    every technical standard, feature, and deployment decision encodes values
-   Campaigns described in these slides succeeded because people acted on this understanding
-   Refusing to engage is itself a choice about who makes decisions and in whose interest
    -   Builders of Facebook's engagement algorithm shaped these political conditions without apparently understanding it [%b Young2024 %]

</section>
