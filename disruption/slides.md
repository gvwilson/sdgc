# Disruption, Fraud, and Enclosure

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Ordinary Business Problems, Illegal Solutions

-   Drug cartels face quality control, territory, supply chain, and labor problems
-   They solve them through violence, bribery, and monopoly enforcement
    -   Sinaloa cartel built quality-control systems: direct response to customer defection [%b Skarbek2014 Wainwright2017 %]
-   Business logic is conventional
-   Only the methods differ

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## What Legal Protections Actually Do

-   Legal protections for workers reduce mistreatment
-   Removing protections, formally or by circumventing them, produces brutality
    -   Workers in cartel-adjacent industries like unlicensed waste hauling or unregistered construction have no recourse for wage theft, injury, or nonpayment
-   Labor conditions of cartels are the labor conditions of unregulated markets

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Disruption as Regulatory Arbitrage

-   "Disruption" often means operating outside the regulatory frameworks that bind legal competitors
-   Uber avoided taxi regulations, Airbnb avoided hotel regulations, gig platforms avoided labor law
    -   Taxi medallions in New York cost up to $1.3M before Uber entered
    -   The regulatory obligations they represented cost Uber nothing [%b Srnicek2016 %]
-   Competitive advantage comes from not following the rules, not from superior innovation

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Externalizing Harm Is a Business Strategy

-   Concentrated firms push costs onto workers, communities, and public services
-   Pollution, unsafe conditions, and underfunded infrastructure are all forms of externalization
    -   Amazon's delivery speeds depend partly on drivers classified as independent contractors who bear vehicle wear, fuel, and injury costs
-   Profitability partly measures how much cost has been transferred to others

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## What Is a Control Fraud

-   [%g control_fraud "Control fraud" %] occurs when those running an institution use that control to loot it
-   The institution is the weapon, not the target
-   Charles Keating and the savings and loan crisis, Enron, and the subprime mortgage system
    -   In each case, fraud consisted of consistently choosing the most aggressive interpretation in every ambiguous accounting decision [%b Black2005 %]

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## How Auditors Miss What Matters

-   Auditors see volume they cannot fully examine
-   Fraudulent managers anticipate audit tests and prepare for them
    -   Enron's CFO Andy Fastow created hundreds of special-purpose entities specifically designed to pass audits while hiding debt [%b Black2005 %]
-   Auditors are financially dependent on the clients they audit

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## The Structures That Make Fraud Predictable

-   Control fraud structures are often visible in advance
    -   Theranos's blood-testing claims required technology that expert engineers said was physically impossible
    -   Company raised $900M before collapse [%b Davies2022 Palazzo2025 Perrow1999 Schneier2023 %]
-   Each layer finds maintaining the deception easier than stopping it
-   Incentive structures, not individuals, explain the pattern

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## The Commons Before Enclosure

-   Medieval English common land governed by community rules, not private ownership
-   Common rights included grazing, fuel collection, and gleaning
    -   Gleaning (right to gather leftover grain after harvest) was formal legal entitlement, not charity [%b Bollier2014 Linebaugh2014 Linklater2015 Ostrom2015 Thompson1963 %]
-   Arrangement sustained communities for centuries before enclosure

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## How Private Property Was Imposed

-   Enclosure acts converted common land to private property through Parliament
    -   Over 5,000 individual enclosure acts passed between 1700 and 1900, covering roughly 7 million acres of English common land [%b Thompson1963 %]
-   Process displaced rural communities and produced the urban labor force for industry
-   Private property in land is younger than assumed
-   Imposed on communities, not discovered as a natural arrangement

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Colonial Law and Collective Tenure

-   Colonial administrations refused to recognize collective land tenure as legitimate property
-   Communally managed land declared "waste" available for appropriation
    -   British colonial law in East Africa, India, and Australia followed the same template: communal use was legally invisible, so communal land could be seized
-   Pattern replicated across Africa, Asia, and the Americas

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## Enclosing Digital Infrastructure

-   Open protocols (email, the web) replaced by proprietary platforms
-   APIs that were open are closed when platforms achieve dominance
    -   Twitter opened its API in 2006 to enable third-party clients and closed it in 2023, charging $100/month for basic access [%b Doctorow2022 %]
-   Logic identical to the enclosure of physical commons

</section>
<section class="slide" markdown="1">

## The Same Logic, New Vocabulary

-   "Disruption," "the sharing economy," and "democratizing access" are enclosure in new language
-   Extracting value from a commons while preventing collective governance is not a new idea
    -   Spotify pays artists fractions of a cent per stream while licensing the entire catalog of recorded human music for its own commercial use
-   The historical record clarifies what is actually happening

</section>
